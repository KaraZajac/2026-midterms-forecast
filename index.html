6 — Method

Per-race priors

Each race has an expected margin (D-share minus R-share, in percentage points) and a race-specific sigma. For House races on the Cook/Sabato/IE radar, we average the three raters' implied P(D wins) and back out a margin assuming the total per-race standard deviation is √(σnat² + σrace²) with σnat = 4 pp and σrace ∈ {1.2, 2.5, 3.5, 4.5} pp depending on rater confidence. For Senate races we use polling-derived margins where available, regressed partially toward fundamentals (state PVI + incumbency).

National environment

Each trial samples a single national swing nat_swing ~ N(0, 4) percentage points. Senate races see a correlated but distinct swing nat_swing_sen = ρ·nat_swing + √(1−ρ²)·N(0,4) with ρ = 0.65. This is the model's most important feature: when the environment moves, races move together, which is why the seat-count distribution has the shape it does rather than being a smooth Gaussian around independent flips.

Race outcomes

For each race in each trial:

margin = expected_margin + nat_swing · elasticity + N(0, sigma_race)
D wins if margin > 0

Elasticity is 1.0 for open seats, ~0.88 for entrenched incumbents (incumbent campaigns absorb some of the national environment).

Why center the national swing at 0 rather than at "fundamentals"?

The Cook / Sabato / Inside Elections ratings updated through May 8–21, 2026 already price in Trump's ~38% approval and the D+6.6 generic ballot. Centering at zero is shorthand for "the May ratings are right on average; uncertainty represents how the environment evolves between now and November plus pollster/rater error." Empirically, the 6-month-out generic-ballot-to-final-popular-vote gap has SD ~3–5 pp, which is where σnat = 4 comes from. The model's headline numbers will shift if you change this assumption (see scripts/montecarlo.js top-of-file constants).

What this model does NOT do

  • It does not model state-level correlated errors (e.g. a regional polling miss in the Sun Belt). All correlation is national.
  • It does not separately model turnout uncertainty. That gets absorbed into σnat and σrace.
  • It does not use district-level head-to-head polls (rare in May for House races; the ratings already absorb what's available).
  • It assumes the 9 redistricted states' new lines are reflected in current ratings. For most of those seats the ratings shifted within days of map adoption; for the late-May Florida and Tennessee maps the ratings are still settling.
  • It does not model RCV second-choice flows in Alaska or Maine — both are flagged with wider sigma.

8 — Caveats

  • This is May, not November. 162 days is a long time. The model is honest about that — σnat = 4 pp produces 80% intervals on the House of roughly ±20 seats. Anyone telling you they know who wins the House in May 2026 is selling something.
  • The ratings are not independent. Cook, Sabato, and Inside Elections look at similar evidence. Averaging them reduces idiosyncratic shop bias but does not produce a fully-independent ensemble.
  • Florida and Tennessee maps are weeks old. The May 4 and May 7 mid-decade redraws will continue to shake out ratings into the summer. Florida-9, Florida-14, and TN-09 in particular may move further as candidate fields settle.
  • The Texas Senate race depends on the May 26 runoff. If Paxton beats Cornyn, the model's R+3 expected margin (which assumes some Paxton-candidate-quality drag) is roughly right. If Cornyn survives, the seat reverts to Likely R / Solid R.
  • Alaska and Maine use RCV. The model treats both as single-round head-to-heads with wider sigma. Round-by-round elimination dynamics could flip either race by 5+ points relative to the first-choice numbers.
  • Nebraska's Osborn is an independent. The model assumes he caucuses Democratic if elected. He has not committed to either side.
  • Vacancies are treated as held by the prior party. CA-01 (LaMalfa, R, deceased) is a near-certain D pickup under Prop 50 redraw; TX-23 vacancy is a near-certain R hold.
