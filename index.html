House — battleground races

The 55 districts where Cook / Sabato / Inside Elections sees meaningful competition or where mid-decade redistricting moved the seat. Sorted by competitiveness. The other 380 districts model as near-certain holds.

Tipping point

Across 5,000 trials, which House district most often sits at the 218th seat — the one whose result determines the chamber? Flipping any of these is worth a percentage point or more on House control probability.

    Method

    The simulation

    20,000 trials. Each trial draws one national environment shift from N(0, 4) percentage points. That shift is then applied to every race (scaled by per-race elasticity). Per-race idiosyncratic noise is added on top. D wins the race if the resulting D−R margin is positive. Seat counts are summed.

    Per-race priors

    For the ~55 House districts and 35 Senate races where Cook / Sabato / Inside Elections gave a rating in May 2026, we average the three raters' implied P(D wins) and back out an expected margin. For Senate races with public polling, we use the polling-derived margin instead, regressed partially toward the state's PVI to account for low-name-ID candidates. For the other 380 House districts we compute the prior from the district's PVI plus a small incumbency bonus.

    Why the line is at 50 for Senate

    50 D-caucus seats is the visual majority line — anything to the right of it means Democrats hold strictly more seats than Republicans. But the underlying model uses 51 for the P(D wins) calculation because the Republican Vice President breaks 50–50 ties for actual control of the chamber.

    What this model does NOT do

    • State-level correlated polling errors (e.g. a Sun Belt-specific miss). All correlation is national.
    • Turnout uncertainty as a separate factor — that gets absorbed into the national-environment and per-race sigmas.
    • Ranked-choice voting dynamics for Alaska and Maine. Both races get wider sigma instead.
    • Louisiana's pending Callais v. Landry Supreme Court ruling on the Black-opportunity district, which could trigger a 2026 redraw.

    Caveats

    • It's May. 162 days out. Anyone who tells you they know who wins Congress in 2026 is selling something.
    • Cook, Sabato, and Inside Elections look at similar evidence. Averaging them reduces idiosyncratic shop bias but is not a fully independent ensemble.
    • Florida and Tennessee mid-decade maps are weeks old. Ratings on the affected districts will keep moving as candidate fields settle.
    • If Cornyn survives the May 26 Texas Senate runoff, TX-SEN reverts toward Likely R; if Paxton wins, the Tilt R / D-leaning trial heats hold.
    • Nebraska's Dan Osborn runs as an independent. The model assumes he caucuses Democratic if elected; he has not committed.
