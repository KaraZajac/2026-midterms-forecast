# The Exceptions: 1934, 1998, 2002

The president's party has gained House seats in a midterm only three times since 1934. Each case illuminates the conditions under which the standard "midterm penalty" inverts.

## 1934 — FDR / Democrats: +9 House, +9 Senate

**Context.** FDR took office March 1933 in the depths of the Depression. By Nov 1934, GDP had grown ~10% from its 1933 trough, unemployment had fallen from ~25% to ~22% (still catastrophic but visibly improving), and the New Deal's first wave (NRA, AAA, CCC, FDIC, Glass-Steagall, TVA) was popular among the working and lower-middle classes most damaged by the Depression.

**Approval.** Gallup did not yet conduct regular presidential approval polls in 1934 (Gallup's polling began in 1935). Inferred approval from later 1935–36 Gallup data and 1936 reelection landslide is in the ~60–70% range.

**Why it was different.**
- The election functioned as a referendum on a *response to crisis* rather than on a stable status quo. Voters rewarded visible action.
- The opposition (Republicans) was associated with Hoover and the Depression; they had no credible counter-message.
- Democrats had won in 1932 with 313 House seats — already a large majority — yet still gained, which is structurally unusual.

## 1998 — Clinton / Democrats: +5 House, 0 Senate

**Context.** The Lewinsky scandal broke January 1998. House Republicans, led by Speaker Gingrich, pushed impeachment proceedings hard through the fall campaign. The economy was booming: GDP growth ~4.5%, unemployment 4.4%, budget moving into surplus.

**Approval.** Clinton's Gallup approval averaged 62–66% in the weeks before the election — the highest midterm approval of any Democratic president since Kennedy.

**Why it was different.**
- **Impeachment backlash.** Alan Abramowitz (Emory, 2001) found the GOP underperformed expected seat share by 10–15 seats in districts where the impeachment campaign was most salient. Voters punished what they saw as overreach.
- **Economy.** Peak-cycle prosperity disconnected economic anxiety from the incumbent party.
- **Pre-existing exposure.** Democrats had lost 52 seats in 1994; many of the most marginal Democratic seats were already gone, leaving a stronger floor.

[Sources: Wikipedia, "1998 United States elections," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1998_United_States_elections; Abramowitz, "It's Monica, Stupid: The Impeachment Controversy and the 1998 Midterm Election," Legislative Studies Quarterly 2001; Kondik (Rasmussen), "The Shadow of 1998," https://www.rasmussenreports.com/public_content/political_commentary/commentary_by_kyle_kondik/the_shadow_of_1998]

## 2002 — G.W. Bush / Republicans: +8 House, +2 Senate

**Context.** First midterm after the September 11, 2001 attacks. The Iraq War authorization passed Congress in October 2002. The national security frame dominated the campaign; Democrats were on defense over the Homeland Security Department reorganization (Bush attacked Senate Democrats for prioritizing union protections).

**Approval.** Bush's Gallup approval was 66–68% in the weeks before the election — the highest midterm approval since Eisenhower 1954. He had peaked at 90% post-9/11 and was still riding the rally.

**Why it was different.**
- **Rally-around-the-flag.** A genuine national security shock displaced the standard "referendum on the president" dynamic.
- **Bush's personal campaigning.** He held 17 rallies in the final week. James Campbell (Buffalo) attributes 3–5 of the 8 House seats to direct presidential coattails — unusual for a midterm.
- **Redistricting.** 2001–02 redistricting (post-2000 Census) net-favored Republicans, especially in Pennsylvania, Michigan, and Florida; structural rather than political contribution of ~3–4 seats.
- **Weak Democratic message.** Daschle/Gephardt offered no national alternative on the war.

## Common features

All three reversals share:
1. **Presidential approval ≥ 60%** at the time of the election (1934 estimated, 1998 and 2002 measured).
2. **A unifying narrative** owned by the president's party — recovery (1934), prosperity + opposition overreach (1998), national security (2002).
3. **A flailing opposition** without a coherent counter-message.

For 2026: Trump's May 2026 approval is ~38% (well below 50%), there is no rally event, and the opposition has a clear economic frame (tariff-driven inflation). None of the exception conditions hold. The base-rate prior for a sub-40% president should govern.
