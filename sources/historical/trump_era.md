# Trump-Era Patterns: 2018, 2020, 2022, 2024

## Summary

Four distinctive features of the Trump era (2016–present):

1. **Senate-presidential alignment collapsed split-ticket voting** to near-zero in 2016 and 2020, then partially rebounded in 2024.
2. **Candidate quality matters more, not less.** Several MAGA-aligned nominees underperformed the partisan baseline of their states/districts by 3–6 points in 2022 and 2024.
3. **Midterm penalties have been amplified for Trump** (-40 House seats in 2018 at 42% approval) and **muted for Democrats opposing him** (Biden lost only 9 House seats in 2022 at 42% approval).
4. **Polling bias is conditional on Trump being on the ballot.** Pro-D bias of 3–5 points in 2016/2020/2024; near zero in 2018/2022.

[Source: Pew Research, "2024 elections show more partisan splits…," https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2024/11/26/2024-elections-show-more-partisan-splits-between-states-presidential-and-senate-votes-than-in-recent-past/; Sabato's Crystal Ball, "The Shocking Decline of Senate Ticket-Splitting," https://centerforpolitics.org/crystalball/the-decline-of-senate-ticket-splitting/]

The 2024 partial rebound matters for 2026 forecasting: it suggests that a few percentage points of cross-pressured voters are recoverable for the right candidates (Brown-OH lost, but Tester-MT, Casey-PA lost narrowly; in 2024 Slotkin-MI, Rosen-NV, Gallego-AZ, Baldwin-WI all won states Trump carried). This is the empirical basis for ~3-point candidate-quality / individualism premiums in 2026 Senate models.

## Candidate quality penalty (CQ)

The classic "Jacobson candidate-quality" measure rewards experienced officeholders (current/former elected officials) and penalizes amateurs. The Trump-endorsement era has created a new sub-category: nominees with low political experience, controversial public positions, or weak vetting.

**2024 underperformance:**
- Kari Lake (AZ Senate) underperformed Trump-AZ by ~4 points.
- Bernie Moreno (OH) outperformed expectations modestly (won by 3.5 in a state Trump won by 11).
- Dave McCormick (PA) ran ~2 points behind Trump.

The empirical CQ premium for a "normal" candidate over a flawed MAGA-aligned candidate, post-2020, is roughly **3–5 points** in competitive Senate races. This is large enough to flip several seats and should be modeled explicitly with a per-candidate adjustment.

## Trump's effect on out-party turnout

The presence of Trump (in office or as a salient figure) appears to mobilize Democratic-leaning voters in midterms beyond what approval ratings alone predict:

- **2018:** Trump approval ~42%, Democrats +8.6 in House popular vote, gained 40 House seats. This is roughly consistent with the historical curve.
- **2022:** Biden approval ~42% (similar to Trump 2018), but Democrats lost only 9 House seats — far better than the curve predicts. Trump's continued salience (Jan 6, his endorsed primary candidates) likely worth 15–25 House seats relative to base case.

If the 2018 vs 2022 asymmetry is real and persistent, it implies a **structural ~1-point Democratic bonus in House popular vote** in Trump-salient midterms (i.e., 2026 likely qualifies because Trump is in office). This is in addition to, not instead of, the standard approval penalty.

## Generic ballot vs presidential approval — Trump-era divergence

In 2018, Democrats led the generic ballot by ~7 points despite Trump's approval being ~42% (a 49-point implied "midterm penalty"); in 2022, Democrats *trailed* by 2.5 despite Biden's approval being ~42% (a ~40-point swing in implied penalty between two similarly-situated cycles). This volatility suggests the approval → generic ballot relationship has weakened in the Trump era; modelers should pool approval + generic ballot as two informative-but-noisy signals rather than treating either as primary.

## Implications for 2026

1. The Trump presidency at 38% approval and below historically predicts a 40+ House seat loss. The 2018 precedent (his only midterm) supports this baseline.
2. Candidate quality may again be decisive in 2026 Senate races (NC-Tillis, ME-Collins, GA-Ossoff, OH-Husted, IA-Ernst).
3. Polling should be calibrated to the midterm baseline (low bias), not the presidential-year baseline.
4. Ticket-splitting capacity is partially restored, especially for entrenched incumbents and well-known Democratic governors-turned-senators (e.g., Sherrod-Brown-style candidates).
