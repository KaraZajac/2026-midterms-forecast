## Historical economic predictors of midterm vote

Political scientists consistently find these variables predictive of national congressional vote share:

### 1. Real disposable income growth (election-year Q2 over Q2)
- Most consistently significant indicator in Hibbs's "Bread and Peace" model, Abramowitz's "Time for Change," and Bartels's work.
- 1-point increase in YoY real disposable income growth → roughly **0.6–0.9 percentage points** of vote share for the president's party.
- 2026 reading (Q1: +1.1%) is weak; Q2 likely similar. Implies modestly negative environment for Republicans on this dimension.

### 2. Inflation (CPI, year-over-year)
- Asymmetrically punitive: high inflation hurts the incumbent party more than equivalent disinflation helps.
- Historical regressions (Bartels 2008, Achen-Bartels 2016) suggest each 1 ppt of CPI above 3% costs the incumbent ~0.5 points of vote share.
- 2026 reading (3.8%) implies roughly a 0.4-point penalty.

### 3. Unemployment level and change
- Levels matter less than *changes*. Each 0.5 ppt increase in U-rate over the election year costs the incumbent ~1 point.
- 2026: U-rate has risen from 3.9% (Jan 2025) to 4.4% (Feb 2026), a +0.5 ppt rise. Implies ~1-point penalty on this dimension.

### 4. Gas prices
- Largest *psychological* effect of any economic indicator (Hamilton 2009, Achen-Bartels 2016). Voters update on gas prices weekly.
- Each $1/gal rise YoY associates with ~2 points of incumbent-party vote share loss.
- 2026: roughly +$0.75 YoY, implying a 1.5-point penalty.

### 5. Consumer sentiment (Michigan ICS)
- Often used as a summary indicator. The 2026 Q1 reading (66, per Michigan Survey) is below 70, historically associated with incumbent-party losses of 4–5% of vote share relative to neutral.

## Composite implied effect for 2026

Adding the four primary channels (with some double-counting risk between gas prices, inflation, and real income):

| Channel | Implied incumbent vote share Δ |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Real disposable income (+1.1%) | −0.5 to −1.0 |
| Inflation (3.8% headline) | −0.4 |
| Unemployment Δ (+0.5 ppt) | −1.0 |
| Gas prices (+$0.75) | −1.5 |
| **Composite (with overlap discount)** | **−2 to −3 points** |

Translation: economic fundamentals alone predict Republicans (the president's party) underperform the partisan baseline by roughly 2–3 points in the 2026 House popular vote. Combined with the approval-based prior of −4 to −6 points, the *fundamentals* implied House popular vote margin is **D+5 to D+7**, which historically translates to roughly a **20–35 seat Democratic gain** in the House.

## Historical models worth replicating

- **Abramowitz "Time for Change":** uses Q2 real GDP growth, June approval, term-incumbency dummy. For midterms, the variant uses approval + economic growth.
- **Hibbs "Bread and Peace":** real disposable income growth per capita over the term, plus cumulative war casualties.
- **Bafumi-Erikson-Wlezien (BEW) generic ballot model:** combines generic ballot with approval and economy.
