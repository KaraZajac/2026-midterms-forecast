# Incumbency Advantage: House and Senate

## Summary

The incumbency advantage — the share of vote a candidate gets purely because they hold the seat, net of partisanship, candidate quality, and national environment — has **declined dramatically since 2000**. It is now roughly **1/3 to 1/2 its 1990s peak**.

| Era | House incumbency advantage |
|-----|---------------------------|
| 1950s | ~2 points |
| 1960s–70s | ~4–6 points |
| 1980s–90s peak | ~8–10 points |
| 2000s | ~6–7 points |
| 2012–2020 | ~3–4 points |
| 2022–2024 | **~2–3 points** |

Sources: Jacobson, "It's Nothing Personal: The Decline of the Incumbency Advantage in US House Elections," Journal of Politics 77(3), 2015, https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/681670; Fiorina, Hoover Institution essay "The Renationalization of Congressional Elections," https://www.hoover.org/sites/default/files/research/docs/fiorina_renationalizationofcongressionalelections_7.pdf; FairVote, https://fairvote.org/shifts_in_incumbency_advantage_in_the_us_house/

## Why it has declined

Three interlocking mechanisms (Jacobson 2015, Abramowitz 2018, Fiorina 2017):

1. **Nationalization.** Voters increasingly treat House races as expressions of national party preference; personal-vote behaviors (constituency service, casework, name recognition) yield less.
2. **Partisan sorting / straight-ticket voting.** Ticket-splitting between president and House has collapsed (see `trump_era.md`). In 2020, only 16 of 435 House districts split their president-House vote — the lowest figure in the modern era.
3. **Reduced redistricting protection.** Independent commissions and court orders have reduced "incumbent protection" gerrymanders in several states (CA, AZ, MI, NY).

## House: current best estimates

- **Jacobson (2015, 2020):** incumbency advantage fell from ~10 points (1986–2000) to ~3 points (2014–2020). His 2020 update finds the *direct personal* advantage is now near zero in highly partisan districts; remaining advantage is concentrated in cross-pressured (lean-R or lean-D, not safe) seats where incumbents can win 3–5 points more than a generic co-partisan.
- **Abramowitz (2018, "The Great Alignment"):** ~2-point average advantage for House incumbents 2014–2018.
- **Fowler & Hall (2014):** Regression-discontinuity estimate of ~3.5 points in 2000s.
- **Erikson (2020):** ~3 points 2018–2020.

**Recommended prior for 2026 House:** incumbent advantage of **2–3 points** in competitive races, with virtually no advantage in safe seats. Open seats should be modeled as the district fundamental (Cook PVI) plus national environment, with no incumbency adjustment.

## Senate: weaker but persistent

The Senate incumbency advantage is harder to estimate (smaller n, longer terms, more idiosyncratic candidates). Best estimates:

- **1980s–90s:** ~6–8 points (Abramowitz/Segal, "Senate Elections," 1992).
- **2010s:** ~3–5 points (Highton & Burris, 2018, *American Politics Research*).
- **2018–2024:** ~2–4 points; clear evidence of decline, with Senate races increasingly tracking presidential vote in their states.

The Senate also exhibits an **incumbent-loss penalty for low candidate quality**: weak challengers facing entrenched incumbents (Schumer-NY, Cornyn-TX) win by larger margins than the partisan structure predicts. Conversely, weak *incumbents* (Heitkamp 2018, McCaskill 2018) can underperform by 5+ points.

**Recommended prior for 2026 Senate:** incumbent advantage of **2–4 points**, with wider uncertainty than House (SD ~2 points on the estimate itself). Apply only in genuinely competitive races; in lopsided states it is absorbed by partisanship.

## Open seats in 2026

The incumbency-decline literature implies open seats are *less* of a special case than they used to be. When the incumbency premium was 10 points, an open seat was a 10-point swing; today it is more like a 2–3 point swing. The "open-seat penalty" for retirements should be scaled down accordingly.
